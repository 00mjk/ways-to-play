Three Men’s Morris is an ancient game for 2 players.

Terminology

It was known in the past by many names in England. Some of them include ‘knockings in and out’.‌1 (20), 2 (321)

Murray‌3 claims that a distinct game called ‘Nine Holes’ existed, but I don’t believe that it was separate to Three Men’s Morris, as all the references seem to track back to a single entry in Robert Nares’ Glossary.‌4 (345)

Writing in 1694,‌5 Hyde said it (or the variant where 3 pieces line up on each side to begin with) was called “Che-Lo” in Chinese (i.e. 直六 zhí liù ‘straight six’1), “Hugjurè” in Persian (probably هجوره “his majesty”, but he also said that each player has 6 pieces), “cashlan gherra” in Irish (i.e. caisleáin gearr ‘short castle’), and “Copped-Crown” in Cumbria & Westmorland.

In other parts of the world, it is known as Tapatan in the Philippines, or 六卒棋 (luk6 zeot1 kei4 ‘six man game’) in southern China.‌6 (648)2

Variants

Circular Three Men’s Morris

See.‌13 ‌9 calls it ‘Shisima’, and says it is played in Kenya.3 It has also been published as ‘Tri-Pin’ by Louis Marx & Co., Swansea, UK.

X-ceter-O

X-ceter-O (or X-für-O in Germany) has 6 pieces numbered #1–#6, alternating between sides. The pieces must be placed or moved in order (and can leap to any unoccupied square), looping back to #1 after #6 has been moved.

King

King (Jim Wilkinson, 1985, Paradigm Games). A commercial implementation. No first play to centre, and pieces may move on the long diagonals.

Chung Toi

Chung Toi (Reginald Chung, 1994, multiple publishers).

Each player’s pieces are octagonal, and have a cross incised upon the top. After the placement phase, a piece can move one or two squares (jumping is allowed) in any of the four directions that the cross is pointing, and may also rotate. A piece may also rotate without moving instead.
